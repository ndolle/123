The regulatory landscape for quantum technology is unique. Unlike mature sectors (like finance or medicine) where strict rules exist, quantum is largely in a **pre-regulatory phase** regarding the *use* of the technology.

Instead, current and upcoming major regulations focus intensely on two themes:

1. **Protective Regulation:** Export controls and investment screening to maintain a national competitive advantage (National Security).
2. **Preparatory Regulation:** Mandates requiring organizations to prepare for "Q-Day"—the future moment when quantum computers can break standard encryption.

Here is an organization of the major regulations in place or upcoming, sorted by key geolocations.

---

### **1. United States (USA)**

The USA is currently the most active regulator, focusing heavily on transitioning critical infrastructure away from vulnerable cryptography and imposing strict trade restrictions.

| Focus Area | Status | Major Regulation / Directive / Standard | Description |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Cryptography Prep** | **In Place** | **Quantum Computing Cybersecurity Preparedness Act** (Public Law 117-260, 2022) | This law requires all federal agencies to inventory their cryptographic systems, prioritize them based on sensitivity, and begin planning the transition to Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC). |
| **National Security** | **In Place** | **Executive Order 14073** / National Security Memorandum 10 (NSM-10) | Directives standardizing cryptographic requirements for national security systems and establishing a plan to maintain US leadership in quantum, while mitigating risks. |
| **Trade Restrictions** | **In Place** | **Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) / EAR Updates** | The Department of Commerce has placed multiple quantum entities (foreign competitors) on the "Entity List," severely restricting their access to US technology. BIS is actively drafting new advanced computing/quantum specific export control definitions. |
| **Investment Controls** | **Upcoming** | **Executive Order 14105 (Outbound Investment)** | Proposes to restrict US persons/capital from investing in certain quantum computing (computing, sensing, communications) technologies in "countries of concern" (specifically China). |
| **Standardization** | **Upcoming** | **NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography Standards** | While technically standards (not laws), NIST’s selection of algorithms for PQC (expected finalization in 2024–2025) will be mandated by FIPS standards, effectively making them the mandatory legal baseline for the US government and the de facto legal baseline for global business. |

---

### **2. European Union (EU)**

The EU is taking a holistic "Strategic Autonomy" approach, seeking to build its own sovereign quantum infrastructure while updating trade rules to match.

| Focus Area | Status | Major Regulation / Directive / Standard | Description |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Trade Restrictions** | **In Place** | **Recast of the Dual-Use Regulation** (Regulation 2021/821) | Updated controls over "dual-use" technologies (items usable for both civilian and military applications), which explicitly include specific advanced manufacturing equipment relevant to quantum processors. |
| **Investment Controls** | **In Place** | **EU Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) Screening Regulation** | A framework encouraging member states to screen foreign investments in "critical technologies," which include quantum computing and advanced semiconductors. |
| **Infrastructure Policy** | **In Place** | **EuroQCI / European Quantum Pact** | A policy initiative binding member states to collectively build a secure, pan-European quantum communication infrastructure. This acts as a soft mandate on telecommunications players to align with European standards. |
| **Standardization** | **Upcoming** | **ETSI QSC Standards** | The European Telecommunications Standards Institute (ETSI) is actively defining standards for Quantum Safe Cryptography, which will likely be incorporated into future EU legal directives (like revisions to NIS2 or the Cyber Resilience Act). |

---

### **3. United Kingdom (UK)**

The UK aims to be an agile, "pro-innovation" regulator, balancing strong security controls with a desire to attract quantum investment.

| Focus Area | Status | Major Regulation / Directive / Standard | Description |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **National Security** | **In Place** | **National Security and Investment (NSI) Act** (2021) | Allows the government to call in, review, and potentially block or condition any foreign investment or acquisition involving 17 sensitive sectors, including specific definitions for quantum technologies. |
| **Strategic Governance** | **In Place** | **National Quantum Strategy** (2023) | While a policy document, it formalizes a pro-innovation, context-based regulatory approach. It empowers sector-specific regulators (like the FCA for finance) to issue guidance on quantum preparedness rather than creating an omnibus "Quantum Law". |
| **Trade Restrictions** | **In Place** | **Export Control Order (2008) updates** | The UK maintains its own strict dual-use and military export lists, regularly updated to capture advanced material and manufacturing capabilities required for quantum development. |

---

### **4. China (PRC)**

China's regulatory landscape is state-driven and opaque, focusing primarily on data localization, infrastructure security, and state adoption of indigenous quantum capabilities.

| Focus Area | Status | Major Regulation / Directive / Standard | Description |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Infrastructure Policy** | **In Place** | **14th Five-Year Plan / National Mandates** | Strong state directives mandate the use of indigenous quantum technology (particularly Quantum Key Distribution, QKD) in critical infrastructure, government finance, and national telecommunications networks. |
| **Security & Cryptography** | **In Place** | **Data Security Law / Cryptography Law** | Mandates how encryption is managed. The Chinese government controls the approval of commercial cryptographic products, and regulations encourage standardizing and migrating to indigenous quantum-resistant standards. |
| **Trade Restrictions** | **Upcoming/Updates** | **Export Control Law** (and related regulations) | Recent updates allow for restrictions on exports based on national security. China has already implemented restrictive licensing requirements for materials critical to advanced computing (like gallium and germanium), which indirectly affects quantum hardware. |

---

### **Summary of Global Regulatory Themes**

* **Export Controls are Synchronizing:** The US, EU, and UK are actively coordinating through forums like the G7 and the EU-US Trade and Technology Council (TTC) to synchronize the technical definitions of what constitutes a "controllable" quantum computer to ensure that restrictions in one jurisdiction are not circumvented in another.
* **Preparatory Mandates are the Urgent Priority for Business:** For general commercial organizations, the **US Quantum Computing Preparedness Act** is the current landmark regulation. Even for non-US companies, any entity that processes sensitive US government data will likely face compliance pressure to begin inventorying their systems and moving toward NIST PQC standards as they go final.
